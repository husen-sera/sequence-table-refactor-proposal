Cost of inactionWhat it costs us to do nothing

If we continue with the current approach, we should expect:

  • Continued fix cycles without permanent resolution. The defect has survived ~23 attempts — there's no evidence the 24th behaves differently. We are renting a fix instead of buying one.
  • More engineering time re-investigating the same area. By our tracking, roughly 115 working days have already been consumed this way — and the meter keeps running.
  • Delayed feature development — every cycle spent here is capacity not spent on customer value.
  • Growing maintenance cost over time — more patches make the area harder to change, so each future fix tends to cost more than the last.
  • Increased regression risk — at a ~1-in-4 break rate, continued patching keeps a new customer-visible incident probable.
  • Slower response to customer issues — today's best case is a multi-day investigation with low odds of a clean fix.
Plain summary: doing nothing is not the zero-cost option. It is the higher-cost option, paid in instalments — with the bill growing over time.

Technical reference (for the engineering team)

What "restructuring" means: the functionality currently lives in a single 4,778-line file mixed with eleven unrelated parts of the app. We reorganize it — without changing what the user sees — into 5–6 small, single-purpose pieces (Layout, Scrolling, Loading, Cards, Grouping & rarities, Sticky elements), built beside the old version behind a feature switch.

Suspected causes (hypotheses, confirmed in Week 1): virtualization window lag, wrong/estimated row heights, async image decode, overlay hiding too early, scroll-restore jump, sticky/offset miscalculation. They share one file today and interfere with each other, which is why they can't currently be isolated.

Parallel work: backend, search/login/settings, the 1-table/2-table views, and bug fixes elsewhere continue (~70% frontend throughput, ~100% backend). New work inside this view is queued to after rollout.

AI acceleration: AI compresses the read/build phases (~6 wks → ~4 wks); equivalence testing and the production soak are calendar-bound and unchanged. AI speeds up writing the change, not reviewing it — the 4-week figure assumes the same testing rigor.
